Are East And West At An Impasse? A Story Told Through The Chinese Semiconductor Industry

Circuit board with a Chinese flag superimposed

By: Mohammad Omar Farooq Ahsan | The University of Manchester

Huawei’s Technological leap with the Mate 60 Pro

Huawei, a leading mobile phone brand from China, recently showcased its advanced Mate 60 Pro. The global tech
community was taken aback to discover that this device is driven by a 7-nanometre (nm) processor. This revelation
has ignited discussions about the true current impact of U.S. sanctions on Chinese advanced semiconductor technology
and how effectively Beijing has managed to navigate around these restrictions.

The 7nm chip, which powers the Mate 60 Pro, is the brainchild of the Shanghai-based Semiconductor
Manufacturing International Corp (SMIC). This achievement is a testament to China's evolving prowess
in the semiconductor domain. Before this innovation, the prevailing assumption, especially considering
the Western sanctions, was that China's technological ceiling was capped at producing 14nm chips.

Understanding the significance of Nanometre Node

In the realm of chip manufacturing, the term 'nanometre node' is pivotal. It essentially denotes the
generation or the technological advancement of the chip. A smaller nm number signifies a more advanced
and efficient chip.

For a historical perspective, chips designed at the 14nm specification made their debut in smartphones
around the mid-2010s. By today's standards, these chips are lagging by two to three generations. Drawing
a comparison, a basic model like the Samsung A20 operates on a 14nm chipset. Currently, the pinnacle of
chip technology hovers around 3nm, predominantly used in high-end smartphones with the only recipient
of this technology being Apple with its home grown A17 Bionic Chip. On the other hand, older and more
established semiconductor chips, which are around 28nm or even larger, find their applications in vehicles
and common household gadgets. Smaller Nanometre chips are required for sophisticated technologies,
predominantly in AI and 5G.

SMIC was only able to mass-produce 14nm chips up until 2022, which, when juxtaposed with global semiconductor
giants Advanced Micro Devices Inc. (AMD) and Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC), who were
producing 7nm chips in 2017, is rather poor. This rapid progression by Huawei and SMIC underscores China's
accelerated efforts to bridge the technological divide.

Dr. Mark Liu, Chairman of TSMC, highlighted the 7nm process as a significant leap and a defining moment in
semiconductor production for the Chinese. The primary distinction between the 7nm and 14nm processes lies
in the substantial increase in the number of transistors per unit area for the 7nm process, coupled with a
marked reduction in energy consumption. This results in 7nm chips being not only more potent than their 14nm
counterparts but also more cost-efficient. For instance, in 2020, the production cost of a 7nm chip stood at
$233. This was not only more economical than the $331 required for a 16nm chip but also surpassed the $238
cost of a 5nm chip. Furthermore, NVIDIA's A100 Tensor Core data-centre processor, built using TSMC's 7nm process,
witnessed a twenty-fold performance enhancement. This optimization meant that a data centre, which previously
necessitated 25 storage racks, could be condensed to just one.

To summarise, 7nm chips offer dual advantages: they reduce ownership costs while simultaneously boosting
computational capabilities. This makes them a financially sound choice for applications in AI, cloud
computing, and 5G, spanning both commercial and defence sectors.

Reassessing the Impact of U.S. Sanctions

While Huawei's advancements are undeniably commendable, it's essential to evaluate the broader picture.
The U.S. sanctions, aimed at curbing China's tech growth, have left tangible dents. Huawei's financials
bear testimony to this. In 2022, their revenue clocked 642 billion yuan (equivalent to US$87.4 billion).
While this might seem impressive, it's a mere shadow of their 2020 revenue, which stood at a whopping
891 billion yuan. Their operating profit too witnessed a slump, hitting a five-year low. While Huawei's
7nm chip is a product of extant technology, their journey towards the next big innovation might be riddled
with challenges, especially without unhindered access to cutting-edge chipmaking technology.

While profits have taken a hit, does it necessarily matter to a company that, in essence, has unlimited
financial and political backing from the CCP, due to its importance for national security and potential
political implications through being a world leader in technological advancement?

China's Pursuit of Semiconductor Autonomy

China's domestic chip production remains limited despite being a significant consumer of the aforementioned.
In 2021, the nation's semiconductor market was valued at approximately $186.5 billion. However, only chips
worth $31.2 billion were produced domestically, resulting in a self-sufficiency rate of 16.7%. Notably,
chips produced by China-based companies constituted just 6.6% of the domestic consumption, valued at
$12.3 billion.

The "Made in China 2025" strategy aims for a 75% self-sufficiency rate by 2030. Given this target, China's
substantial subsidies and policy incentives for semiconductor companies are understandable. Despite some
notable failures in semiconductor development, China remains steadfast in its commitment to bolstering its
semiconductor industry.

Limited Progress

Previously, SMIC's most advanced known chip was the 14nm, as Washington's restrictions in late 2020 prevented
it from acquiring an EUV (used to print the tiny features that make up a microchip) machine from the Dutch
company, ASML. However, according to TechInsights, it seems SMIC might have achieved the production of 7nm chips
by adapting the more basic DUV (same as EUV but less powerful), which they could still acquire from ASML without
restrictions.

Regardless of the chip's manufacturer, Tilly Zhang, an analyst at Gavekal Dragonomics, minimized the achievement.
She pointed to a low yield rate, which decreases the number of functional chips extracted from each wafer,
thereby increasing costs. Additionally, new export controls from the Netherlands will further restrict SMIC's
ability to access advanced immersion DUV machines. Zhang commented, "The only reason Huawei can afford to sell
phones with these chips at standard market prices is due to its vast financial resources combined with substantial
government subsidies."

Recent reports from Reuters indicate that China plans to introduce a new state-supported investment fund targeting
approximately $40 billion for its semiconductor sector, intensifying its efforts to level with the U.S. and other
competitors. These subsidies are of the utmost importance as the Chinese chip industry depends on them for their
survival and ability to compete, as some research entities predict that SMIC's 7nm process might have a yield rate
below 50%, in contrast to the industry standard of 90% or higher. Doug Fuller, a semiconductor researcher at the
Copenhagen Business School, remarked "The U.S. restrictions have significantly increased the costs of producing
controlled technologies in China," suggesting that the Chinese government is likely bearing these expenses.

The Complex Web of the Tech Ecosystem

The semiconductor landscape isn't just about individual companies or nations; it's a complex web of collaborations,
partnerships, and shared innovations. The U.S. CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 adds another layer of complexity.
This act essentially bars beneficiaries of a massive US$52.7 billion incentive program from entering any form
of collaborative research or tech licensing with Chinese entities, especially if it pertains to high-tech
semiconductors.

This poses significant challenges, especially considering the intricate nature of the semiconductor industry.
A case in point is the outsourced assembly, testing, and packaging (OSAT) process. Samsung offers a vivid analogy,
likening the semiconductor to the human brain, with its packaging representing the intricate nervous system and
skeletal structure. The final phase, known as the package test, is a rigorous quality check ensuring the chip's
optimal performance.

Collaborating with Chinese firms in this intricate domain could potentially jeopardize a company's future
collaborations and access to U.S. technological innovations. Moreover, the U.S.'s deficiency in domestic
OSAT capabilities might introduce unforeseen security vulnerabilities. Hence Samsung has limited and, in
some cases, stopped selling advanced chip equipment to their Chinese counterparts to avoid the ire of the
Americans.

China’s Counter strategy: Techno-Nationalism

China, fully aware of the U.S.'s aggressive tech-driven strategies, has adopted a robust counterstrategy.
They are rallying their entire societal fabric against what they perceive as foreign adversaries aiming
to stifle their growth. This sentiment was echoed by China's Ministry of State Security, which recently
issued warnings against potential espionage threats and urged collective vigilance.

This narrative extends seamlessly into the tech domain. The underlying message is clear: Western powers
are attempting to suppress China's technological ascent, and it's the patriotic duty of every Chinese
citizen to thwart these efforts.

In line with this, a Reuters report highlighted China's rejuvenated efforts under its Thousand Talents Plan.
This initiative aims to attract global tech experts, offering them lucrative incentives, all in a bid to
bolster China's tech capabilities and navigate the maze of U.S. restrictions.

Strategic Implications for the Semiconductor Industry

Historical trends in China's electronics sector have informed policymakers that, despite current technological
and production scale gaps with international counterparts, there are strategic advantages to supporting domestic
semiconductor companies. Firstly, a surge in Chinese manufacturers can potentially saturate the market,
limiting opportunities for second and third-tier wafer foundries. Projections suggest that by 2024, China
will lead global chip factory construction, with 31 new facilities, surpassing Taiwan's 19, and the U.S.'s 12.
While these new factories will primarily focus on established process nodes, this strategy could pressure other
manufacturers specializing in these processes.

Secondly, if select Chinese companies excel amidst the policy-supported foundries, they could potentially
challenge or even lead the advanced process market. This model has been successful in sectors like PC/laptop
manufacturing with Lenovo and telecommunications with Huawei and ZTE. SMIC, with its advancements in the 7nm
process, exemplifies a Chinese firm poised to challenge international semiconductor leaders and potentially
bypass U.S. technological constraints.

China's Technological Aspirations and Implications

Historically, U.S. sanctions restricted Chinese chip designers from leveraging TSMC's advanced processes.
If SMIC's 7nm process becomes widely adopted by other Chinese manufacturers, it could catalyse China's
progress in areas like AI, high-speed computing, and 5G. This progression aligns with China's vision of
transitioning from a manufacturing behemoth to a global manufacturing powerhouse.

While specifics about SMIC's 7nm shipments remain undisclosed, advancements in this domain could propel
China's innovations in AI and high-speed computing. Such progress could enhance China's economic and defence
capabilities, posing challenges for Taiwan and the broader East Asian region.

China's modernization goals, set for the People's Liberation Army's centennial in 2027, emphasize
advancements in AI, quantum computing, and hypersonic, all of which necessitate advanced chips. Merely
restricting China's access to EUV machines won't impede its progress in advanced chip processes, which
could bolster its military capabilities.

U.S. Diplomatic Endeavours

The U.S. is currently lobbying Japan and the Netherlands to expand the existing EUV embargo to encompass
DUV machines. While some argue that isolating China might hasten its path to self-sufficiency, historical
patterns suggest China's ambitions won't wane until it achieves market dominance. Hence, merely restricting
China's access to EUV machines might not effectively prevent its advancements in cutting-edge technology
nodes below 10nm.

Peer into the Future

Predicting the trajectory of China's semiconductor sector is akin to gazing into a crystal ball. While financial
prowess is a significant asset, the broader chipmaking ecosystem demands a blend of individual creativity,
innovation, and an environment fostering free expression. These attributes are often synonymous with
democratic setups.

To provide some context, the 2022 Bloomberg Innovative Index ranked the top 10 most innovative countries,
all of which are democracies. In the Asian context, South Korea and Singapore lead the pack, ranking sixth
and seventh respectively. China, despite its massive strides, ranks 11th globally and third in Asia.

While China has been a global frontrunner in patent submissions since 2011, the challenge lies in converting
volume to value and ensuring global applicability and acceptance of these patents.

While the success China achieves in the semiconductor industry can be anyone’s guess due to external and internal
factors, what can be a certainty is that the once prosperous world of collaboration, encouraged by all the developed
nations is slowly coming to an end, being instead replaced by protectionism and bipolarity akin to the cold war,
except this time the nations with the preponderance of power are the U.S.A. and China.
