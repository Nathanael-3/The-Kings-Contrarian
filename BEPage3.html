The Threat Of Rising National Debts

US Capitol building with dollar bills in the background

By: Colin Yin | Harvard University

National debts are rising worldwide with many of the largest economies having debts in excess of 100% of gross
domestic product (GDP). Japan for example has a debt-to-GDP ratio of 263%, whilst the US is at 123%. National
debt is defined as the total sum of money that the government owes countries, companies, and individuals that
it has decided to borrow money from. Once this national debt exceeds GDP like in the two examples previously
given, a country finds itself in a situation where it owes more in debt than it makes in income. In fact,
debts like this are incredibly hard to pay off as they require either astronomically high taxes, which would
drive down GDP as businesses and individuals leave your nation, or consistent fiscal surpluses, which would
see massive cuts to state spending, a measure that is largely political suicide.

Furthermore, the situation of large debts is set to continue as the Congressional Budget Office predicts that
US national debt will reach 195% of GDP in 2053. Whilst for the UK the Office for Budget Responsibility predicts
a national debt around 300% of GDP by 2070. Astronomically high debts like these will have severe consequences
for the economic and political health of these nations and the international system writ large.

As populations across the globe demand ever further increases in government spending on welfare, the military,
and infrastructure, the government must find the money from somewhere in order to fund these programs. This money
can be raised in two ways, through high levels of taxation or through borrowing money or what usually is the case
a combination of the two. However, as governments have continued to borrow money especially during the Covid-19
pandemic, interest payments on debt as a percentage of government expenditure continues to rise. This further
reduces the amount of money that governments have to spend, as larger and larger amounts of tax revenue are set
aside to solely pay off debt, requiring an increasing reliance on borrowing to fund state programs fuelling a
cycle of even more debt.

This current situation seems to have no end, as Congress just recently raised the debt ceiling for the 78th time
since 1960, at the risk of a government default if they hadn’t. This is not a sustainable solution and one that
makes me question whether there is any point to having a debt ceiling if it just continues to get raised. Yet,
both Republicans and Democrats seem to not have the political gusto to actually address this issue in any meaningful
way, with former Speaker of the House Paul Ryan’s social security spending cuts plan, being lambasted by former
President Donald Trump and the majority of the Republican base as one example.

Yet, if no solution is brought forward, the US risks its place as the current global hegemon. An increased reliance
on borrowing decreases the reliability of a repayment on this debt. Much like if you were to borrow money and never
pay it back, lenders would be far less likely to lend you money in future. The same can be said with national debt.
Whilst currently the US is in a more fortunate position than most, being the global hegemon and having the global
reserve currency, providing it with more leeway over its debt levels because the international system relies on the
health of the US government and its legal tender. However, if the US debt continues to spiral out of control the trust
that is required in the US for the dollar to be the global reserve currency will slowly slip away, being replaced
potentially by gold, the euro, the renminbi or another form of currency. This could be made even worse if the financials
of the US government make it such, that heavy cuts have to be made to the US military, decreasing the US’s ability to
take on the role as global hegemon.

In desperate times, governments may have to fall back on money creation in order to pay off this debt. Money creation
involves the printing of money by central banks in order to increase the money supply and incentivise consumption,
as well as to pay off debts. Yet, when the money supply expands, intended to pay off government debt, this causes an
upward inflationary effect driving up the prices of goods and services. This also inadvertently collapses trust in
government bonds, as inflation causes them to lose their value, preventing the government from selling additional
debt to finance state spending. However, most worryingly is that this policy doesn’t even have to be enacted for the
consequences to appear.

If those that hold the dollar suspect that the US will have to, or could potentially, use money creation to pay off
debt they may well begin to sell their holdings of dollars, predicting potential inflation and currency depreciation.
This could create a run on the dollar resulting in stagflation similar to the situation faced under Nixon in the 1970s.
Stagflation is a situation in which high inflation is accompanied by stagnant growth and high unemployment.

A run on the dollar would reduce the pounds buying power and make imports more expensive, which, given the US is an
import oriented economy, particularly with regards to goods, higher import costs mean inflation rises.

As inflation rises, so do interest rates and as a result consumption is disincentivised. This means companies are
less able to sell goods and services, hampering economic growth. Lastly, as economic growth stagnates, firm’s profit
margins will decrease, due to lower demand and as a result firms will begin to lay off workers to keep prices low,
with the aim of increasing demand, resulting in stagflation.

Therefore if the government decides the way forward with regards to paying off debt is to print money, the potential
for high inflation becomes very real. Yet, even more worryingly is the fact that money does not even have to printed
for there to be a run on the dollar and resulting stagflation, if the options open to the US government to pay off
debt shrinks to solely money creation.

Instead, the US has some options to reverse this doom and gloom. A key solution would be to cut state spending heavily,
whereby the US government runs a fiscal surplus every year. The current US deficit stands at $1.5 trillion, which is
larger than the Spanish economy and to reverse, would require a mix of cuts in state spending and higher tax revenue
over a long period of time, coupled with improving economic growth and productivity gains. Unfortunately, the latter
are hard to achieve and the former are politically unpopular. That being said President Jackson was able to pay off
government debt and President Coolidge came as close as any modern president to doing so, cutting national debt by 23%
in six years. These solutions require action now but the current US political situation is apathetical to these
propositions. Maybe, as Warren Buffet argued, the best way to reduce the government deficit and reduce national debt
as a result, is to pass a law that states, if there is a deficit that year of over 3% of GDP, then all sitting members
of congress are ineligible for re-election. That would establish the right incentives.
