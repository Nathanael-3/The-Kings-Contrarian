The Ultimate Pursuit Of Knowledge: Mathematics, Philosophy, And History

The ancient Greek mathematician Euclid as depicted in Raphael's School of Athens

By: Marcus Johansson | The University of Texas at Austin

College is about the application of knowledge in the pursuit of truth. Yet, this mission has largely
gone astray from campuses across America, as college becomes increasingly about credentials, politics,
and incessant specialisation. Our Founding Founders were not interested in having expertise in one
narrow field, but wanted to try their hand at politics, law, philosophy, architecture, engineering,
and the natural sciences. However, to try their hand at so many disparate subjects they required a
strong foundation in how to attain and apply knowledge across disciplines. We called these types of
people Renaissance men. Unfortunately, they have come to be a rather endangered species.

I propose a new academic degree, devoted to the concept of attaining and applying knowledge across
disciplines, and providing young people with the best possible tools to understand how to think.
This academic degree would be three pronged, much like the famous Philosophy, Politics and Economics
course at the University of Oxford. Instead, it would be Mathematics, Philosophy and History,
establishing a degree that encapsulates analytical, theoretical, and empirical ways of thinking.
I believe that these three subjects are integral to technological innovation and the creation of a
free and prosperous society.

Yet, the number of young people interested in any of these three subjects is tragically low.
Mathematics is the 26th most popular major in the US, philosophy is the 56th, and history is 15th.
In fact, when asking my peers about mathematics, philosophy, and history, most of them say these
subjects are useless and donâ€™t teach any practical skills, like business or law do. However, my
response is always to illustrate that our laws come from philosophical ideas and historical context
and our business strategies are filled with mathematical formulas, rooted in philosophical and
historical observations about human nature. AI for example, is a practical piece of technology but
its societal implications are rooted in philosophy.

Mathematics is the study of numbers, utilised in formulas to help explain the physical realities
around us. Philosophy builds on this foundation to theorise about the purpose of these physical
realities, and history provides us with the case studies for these philosophical ideals. When
thought of in this way, the trio of disciplines becomes highly linked from one to the other,
helping us to explain and better the physical realities that we are exposed to in our everyday
lives as humans.

Our progress has largely stagnated in recent years, particularly outside the world of bits.
The world of atoms looks highly similar to what it would have looked like in the 1970s, and
my theory for why we are so focused politically on cultural issues, is because of the intense
level of stagnation that the US faces outside of this sphere. If we are to truly progress, not
in the progressive sense, but in the technological and epistemological sense, we need theorisers,
dreamers, and radicals and there is no better way to teach these characteristics than through
mathematics, philosophy, and history.
