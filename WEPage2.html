A Short Exposé On US-China Relations

A US and Chinese flag in front of a painting of the Great Wall of China

By: Jason Tu | University College London

The US and China have a long and rich history that in recent years has meant an extraordinary amount of
economic collaboration. However, both the US and China have turned towards protectionism as their
relationship has tensed. In this short exposé, I will discuss the events leading up to the present situation
and give my thoughts on the future outlook of US-China relations.

To understand why current geopolitical tensions are so high between these two economic powerhouses, we shall
go back to 2018. The Trump Administration had implemented sweeping tariffs on over US$34 billion worth of
Chinese goods, alongside other trade restrictions in the hopes of forcing China to make changes to what was
deemed their unfair trade practices and intellectual property theft. This sparked a trade war, whereby
China retaliated by placing their own respective tariffs on more than 500 US products. What resulted was
the US being accused of “trade bullying” and China of “ripping off” Western countries.

This trade war, partnered with the Covid-19 outbreak and China’s threats of a Taiwanese invasion, means
that US-China relations have not been this tense since the end of the Cold War. To make matters worse,
Biden recently met with Vietnam’s general secretary, Nguyen Phu Trong where the pair discussed the threat
that China poses and a commitment to a historic US-Vietnam Comprehensive strategic partnership. Given the
manufacturing threat that Vietnam poses for China due to its low labour and capital costs, China is likely
to not take kindly to the US’s attempts to establish allies in the Sino sphere of influence.

Furthermore, both sides are pursuing a more aggressive foreign policy in a time where the US’s relative
global power is weakening and China’s export stability falling. Given the weakened position of both parties,
the two nations are becoming increasingly paranoid, despite both sides making it apparent that geopolitical
fallout serves neither of their interests. That being said, China’s Belt and Road Initiative and the US’s
courting of allies across Asia, partnered with the increased economic warfare conducted by both sides, mean
that US-China paranoia is anything but baseless.

The Biden Administration has recently passed the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act, which focuses on providing US
companies the ability to receive government subsidies, if their respective product and/or business model
promotes a domestic green energy supply or green energy transition. This policy is a three-pronged act,
aimed at reducing high levels of inflation, catalysing the shift away from fossil-fuel energy consumption,
and fostering US-based technological advancement. Yet, this has not changed the global competitive landscape
that the West faces, where China is also vying to be the tech capital of the world, with a particular focus
on electric vehicle manufacturing. The many upcoming US startups such as SilaNano, who manufacture silicon
anodes for electric vehicle batteries and QuantumScape, who are leaders in manufacturing solid-state batteries
all face a common barrier to the green transition and that is China.

China currently leads the electric vehicle industry by possessing a huge market, unprecedented state subsidies
and dominating supply chains over key materials such as lithium, graphite, and copper. Electric vehicle
technology may have been conjured up in the West, but China moved and scaled up early to consolidate its
leadership. According to Allied Market Research, the global electric vehicle industry was valued at $163.01
billion in 2020 and is projected to reach $823.75 billion by 2030, registering a compound annual growth rate of
18.2%. With an astonishingly huge domestic market, the only way to outcompete China is to leapfrog their current
technological prowess. This does not mean minor improvements on the lithium-ion battery but it means replacing
this technology with state-of-the-art innovations.

China’s newfound role as a superpower has created a US response that is rather similar to that opted for by
the Nixon Administration during the 1970s, as Japan began to rise economically. During that time, a trade war
never led to any actual conflict and was unlikely to do so given Japan’s inability to have a large scale army or
nuclear weapons capabilities, yet in the case of China the situation is radically different. A key reason why
conflict between the two superpowers has remained solely economic thus far, has been the mutual economic
interdependence between both nations that make any type of military conflict a costly endeavour that neither
side would gain anything from. Yet, a return to a mercantilist economic approach that abandons free trade as
opted for under both the Trump and Biden administrations, makes the economic upsides of military warfare
potentially greater than its downsides. That being said, given both nations have nuclear weapons capabilities,
the theory of mutually assured destruction makes potential military conflict largely off the table. As a
result, officials on both sides have made it clear that a war is not what they want.

The US and China need to implement policies that aim to reduce the current economic and geopolitical tensions
and focus on increased cooperation on pressing issues that are global in nature, such as AI and climate change.
Failure to do so on issues that drive their current confrontation will inevitably lead to collision. A war
between the world’s largest exporter and the world’s largest economy would be catastrophic and destabilize global
stability for years to come. A reversion back to the free-trade policies of old would be a welcome step away
from this hypothetical.
