Do You Feel Represented? A Case for British Localism

View of an idyllic British village

By: The Editorial Board | 11/09/2023

The UK is made up of 650 constituencies across its four countries, each with different political persuasions,
needs, and desires. Yet, the power that constituencies have to shape their local communities through taxation
to education to now even speed limits is non-existent.

When compared to other Western nations the UK is remarkably centralised, despite devolution. States in the US,
Canada, Germany, Switzerland, the Nordics etc. all have a level of autonomy that UK counties don’t even come
close to. For example, in Sweden healthcare is coordinated on the local level, whilst in Germany the same can
be said of education, and in Switzerland even its executive branch is shared between the different executives
of each canton.

When local politics becomes more influential than national politics, the reigning national political party,
whilst still important, becomes a less polarising issue and surrenders the responsibility to impossibly satisfy
the needs of all 650 constituencies.

Instead of Westminster proclaiming the need to level up the UK, particularly parts of the North, why not give
Northern constituencies more power to establish policies that they believe best suit them? Much like in the US,
where Florida and California are pitched as the two ways to do governance or in Bavaria where business friendly
policies allow it to outperform other German states, for the North to actually improve it has to have the ability
to differentiate itself from London and the South and with the current centralisation of politics, it is unable
to do so.

However, when discussing this idea amongst friends and relatives, people are quick to bemoan the incompetency
of their local MP or local council, insisting that they themselves could probably do a better job. Despite this,
many local MP’s, particularly where I live, have been in power for a very long time as their area continues to
vote along party lines.

This has resulted in local politics becoming completely irrelevant to its locality, merely representing which
party the area would like in the House of Commons. Whilst this is important, there is very little accountability
on the local level, as for example a Labour MP in Lewisham Deptford knows they will never get voted out of office
and thus has no incentive to rock the boat on policy, out of potential fear of things going wrong.

If local MP’s were held more accountable through added power to implement significant policy change on the local
level and operated outside of a two party system, added political competition would bring much-needed alternatives
to the current political status quo. A status quo that is resulting in fewer people turning out to vote and an
increase in the popularity of the phrase ‘politically homeless’ particularly amongst Gen Z.

Finally, the more we abstract politics up the chain to central planners, the more things are likely to go wrong or
nothing to get done whatsoever. Instead, we must reclaim control over our localities to best aid the local communities
that we all reside in. Democracy is not about power but about representation, which is best expressed on the local level.

When politics becomes about power, the executive branch evidently turns into a political war to ideologically
control the lives of the other half that didn’t vote for your candidate. This system polarises the electorate and
leads everyone to feel dissatisfied, feeling as though their areas are neglected from the political spotlight or
that the solutions being proposed are solutions that they vehemently disagree with.

Our current politics is calling for more unity, which can only be accomplished by providing local communities
with the mandate to have autonomy over their own governance.
