It's Time We Privatised The NHS

Hospital reception with nurse walking down a corridor

By: Stephen Pina | University of St Andrews

With median waiting times at thirteen weeks and increasingly common horror stories of those with serious
illnesses having to wait months or even years for treatment, it is evident that the NHS is in need of reform.

This stark reality was on full display during the Covid-19 pandemic, where Britain was forced to lockdown
in an attempt to prevent the inevitable overrun of the NHS, causing many to lose their jobs, commit suicide
and for the government to spend £70 billion that it didn’t have on a furlough scheme.

Now it is the case that many of the poorest in the UK have to opt for private healthcare, paid for through
crowdfunding campaigns, in order to get their necessary treatment in time. Yet the prevailing sentiment remains
resistant to large-scale reform, insisting any issue is a result of chronic underfunding or that privatisation
will result in millions of Brits losing access to healthcare; neither could be further from the truth.

Firstly, the UK spends around 10% of its GDP on healthcare, roughly equal to the Netherlands and more than Denmark,
Australia, Finland, New Zealand, Italy and South Korea (all of which provide their citizens with better health
outcomes than the NHS). Only 10 countries in the OECD spend more on their healthcare, and with the exception
of the US, all are within 1.5% of the UK. Secondly, the NHS budget has increased by roughly £55 billion since
2011, whilst patient outcomes, waiting times and the average length of GP appointments have all got worse,
indicating we are getting less quality for more money.

Furthermore, a recent study conducted by The King’s Fund found that there is no evidence of widespread privatisation
in the NHS, despite persistent claims to the contrary. Those who insist the NHS is on the path to becoming a
US healthcare model are acting in bad faith, by presenting a fallacious argument where the sole privatised
health system example is the US, and the NHS is presented as some sort of utopian counter. Yet, whilst the
US does not have universal coverage, their health outcomes still outperform the UK and thus the challenge becomes
creating a health system that is universal, high quality, and affordable.

The Euro Health Consumer Index ranks Switzerland and the Netherlands as the two best healthcare systems in Europe,
with the UK in 16th. Furthermore, when calculating the average OECD rankings of a group of ten nations,
(South Korea, Switzerland, Australia, Italy, New Zealand, Finland, the Netherlands, Denmark, the US and the UK)
for a range of factors like cancer survival rates, infant mortality, life expectancy, and outcome of cardiovascular
disease care, the top three nations are South Korea, Switzerland and Australia, the Netherlands is fifth and the UK
finishes dead last.

Switzerland, the Netherlands and South Korea all operate universal, cost-effective and quality healthcare that is
primarily privatised, whilst Australia maintains a public-private health system. The Swiss and Dutch systems work
through mandatory private insurance for all citizens and for those who cannot afford, subsidised insurance from the
government. The government also regulates insurance providers, thus preventing price hikes, discrimination and poor
quality control.

This allows for the efficiency, innovation and quality created by the private sector and the universality and
affordability guaranteed by some state intervention. A common-sense middle ground is therefore clearly possible,
where quality healthcare is provided without the catastrophised scenario of millions of Brits losing access to
healthcare.

A further hindrance is the fact that the UK does a terrible job of retaining its top medicine graduates or encouraging
its own population to go into medicine, as most either opt to go overseas for more lucrative salaries upon graduation
or decide to study a subject with more lucrative job prospects. In fact, whilst in the Netherlands only about 3% of
doctors are from overseas; in the UK that figure is almost 30% to meet the domestic shortage.

By privatising the healthcare sector, the UK can benefit from better doctors and more doctors, decreasing waiting
times and increasing health outcomes. As the NHS continues to lag behind its OECD peers, the case for its privatisation
has never been stronger.
