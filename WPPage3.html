The Untapped Conservative Demographic

William F. Buckley meeting with President Richard Nixon

By: Mckenzie Rostack | Princeton University | 06/10/2023

Increasingly, the American right has been supportive of an argument premised on restricting immigration
to the US. Whilst there are credible arguments for restricting immigration, particularly illegal immigration,
I plan to illustrate that if conservatives were more thoughtful and nuanced when addressing this issue amongst
others, there is a huge untapped conservative demographic amongst these immigrant communities, that could allow
the Republicans to win the popular vote for the first time since 2004.

Despite Ruy Teixeira and John Judis’s claims in 2002 about an emerging democrat majority amongst college educated
whites and ethnic minorities, both Hispanics and Asians are actually shifting more right than they are left.
Venezuelans fleeing the effects of socialism in their home country could easily become a major voting bloc for
the Republican Party, much like the Cuban diaspora already is. In fact, most Hispanic, Asian, African, Caribbean,
Middle Eastern and European immigrants to the US tend to lean heavily conservative, particularly on social issues.
It is thus astoundingly poor from a political standpoint that the Republicans are still unable to get these
groups onside.

I theorise that this is for two key reasons: economic interventionism and lacklustre messaging. Public attitudes
towards free-market economics are very low and any positivity around capitalism is hard to come by. A key reason
why Trump was both the nominee and elected in 2016 was because of his willingness to embrace protectionism and
government intervention in the economy. This appealed to working class people of all races and allowed the
Republicans to do significantly better amongst ethnic minorities, stealing many voters who backed Bernie Sanders
in the Democrat primaries. That being said, the Republican Party still attempts to message that they are open to
fiscal conservatism and free-market economics, despite the backtracks made when in office.

As a result, the party promising huge increases in social security, tax-funded healthcare and the like, do
significantly better with a voting base who favours economic interventionism and doesn’t really care for or
think about, the long term effects to massive amounts of government spending. Therefore, Republicans can either
full-scale abandon their commitments to right wing economic policies or they must do a better job at messaging
its benefits to voters.

This poor messaging is apparent outside of economic policy too. Whether its affirmative action, Roe v. Wade,
or immigration, the Republican Party can make huge inroads with religious conservative ethnic-minority communities,
if they were more thoughtful and nuanced in their dispositions. How is it that the vast majority of Asian-Americans
continue to back and vote for a party that openly supports their discrimination or a majority of religious
Hispanic-Americans backing a party that is antithetical to traditional religious ideas and practice?

Conservatives must engage in and win the battle of ideas in these communities. There is a base of conservatism
to be found, which just needs the correct messaging to bring out. Stop engaging in intraparty warfare, election
denial, and Keynesian economics and get back to basics to establish a coalition of ethnically diverse conservatives,
that won’t lose a popular vote for years to come.
