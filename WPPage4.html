Affirmative Action, DEI And What It All Means For The UK

An anti affirmative action protestor in front of the US Supreme Court building

By: Saket Mistri | University of Oxford

In the US, since Regents of the University of California v. Bakke in 1978 the use of race-based affirmative
action was permitted, under the presumption that universities with students from diverse racial backgrounds,
encourages diversity of thought and thus improves educational experience. This was the first time that the
word ‘diversity’ came to replace the word discrimination.

Since then, the word diversity has spread to the corporate sector, NGO’s, governments, and the UN. The word
has gained so much traction that it is increasingly difficult to find a university or business that doesn’t
tout their diversity, equity and inclusion (DEI) initiatives and statistics. The argument being that a
diversity of races, genders, sexualities etc. create an environment that is inclusive to diversity of thought
and this diversity of thought benefits educational outcomes, profit, political outcomes etc.

Yet, is any of this true or are these companies and institutions simply advancing ideological objectives?

Not that much research has actually been done on this subject and what research there is, is often associated
with partisan groups who are already heavily in favour of DEI and affirmative action for reasons other than
diversity of thought and seek to legitimise the practice further, by mentioning a perceived additional benefit.
Instead, from my own experience and the experience of my peers, as well as the situation that is occurring
on university campuses and businesses across the Anglosphere, it seems that DEI and affirmative action initiatives
are having the opposite effect to creating a diversity of thought.

Professors have been fired for voicing their opinion, employees have been silenced, and researchers stifled.
If a movement is attempting to silence any criticism, it is highly unlikely that it promotes a diversity of thought.
In fact, a group of natural scientists (including two Nobel laureates) and various research studies by major think
tanks have both found that there is an inverse link between DEI and diversity of thought, typically because those
who support DEI and benefit from it share a very similar worldview.

This worldview believes group disparity equates to group discrimination and thus group discrimination is necessary
to correct group disparity. Given that criticism of the movement comes with a steep cost and those who advance
DEI tend to share a similar progressive worldview, it instead creates a culture of groupthink that is openly
antagonistic to diversity of thought. In practice, it acts in a very similar way to Herbert Marcuse’s idea of
liberating tolerance.

In fact, it is this belief in group disparity and the need for group discrimination that is the actual driving
force behind affirmative action and DEI initiatives, whilst diversity of thought is merely used a nice sounding
placeholder. The argument for diversity of thought breaks down rather quickly upon the realisation that skin
pigment has no relation to cognitive capabilities, and thus whilst white people have a wide array of diverging
opinions on many different issues and are not one monolith, the same can be said for black people and other races.
Instead, the current cultural milieu punishes companies or individuals who do not partake in this group
discrimination and those who are most fervently pushing for these measures often overcome the lacklustre pushback
of those who are apolitical or socially liberal.

As Justice Thomas states in his concurring opinion on Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard “Harvard and UNC
ask us to blind ourselves to the burdens imposed on the millions of innocent applicants denied admission because
of their membership in a currently disfavoured race.” Thus diversity becomes divvying up individuals by immutable
characteristics, equity becomes discrimination against innocent individuals by dint of their membership to an
unfavourable identity group, and inclusion becomes silence all that oppose this programme. It’s a clever use of
positive language to hide insidious motives, reminiscent of something you might find in Nineteen Eighty-Four.

That being said I don’t believe the majority of those who support these initiatives are acting insidiously,
instead it is more likely coming from a place of compassion or ignorance. The use of words that we deem positive
like ‘inclusion’ and ‘diversity’ mean those who are less politically interested or aware think of these
initiatives in a positive light, especially when partnered with phrases like ‘we just want everyone to feel
included.’ However, the end of affirmative action in the US will mean that companies and institutions will have
to reconsider their use of DEI initiatives, potentially allowing for a deeper discussion on its stated purposes.
Yet, in the UK these initiatives are anything but on there way out. This Conservative government has done little
to counter this worrying trend, even after state institutions like the RAF placed a pause on hiring white males
and almost every company makes a conscious effort to achieve racial and gender parity through discrimination
(both illegal practices).

Yet outside of merely their illegality, these initiatives will make UK firms perform worse than their American
counterparts, due to the fact that hiring based on meritocratic principles allows firms to employ from a wider
talent pool, hire more productive and more talented workers, avoid the needless politicisation of their companies,
and lower their costs by disbanding their DEI programs and firing the people working on them. At a time when the
UK economy is performing badly, ensuring businesses are running as efficiently as possible is in the interest of
the UK government.

Whilst universities like LSE have already been quick to lament the end of affirmative action and its consequences
for business, any litigation brought against a company in the US is unfortunately unlikely to significantly effect
DEI initiatives in the UK and thus UK business practices are unlikely to impacted at all.

Those arguing for a continuation of these programs, I would take heed of the warning of Justice Thomas when he
argues “I would have thought that history had by now taught a “greater humility” when attempting to “distinguish
good from harmful uses of racial criteria.” From the Black Codes, to discriminatory and destructive social welfare
programs, to discrimination by individual government actors, bigotry has reared its ugly head time and again. Anyone
who today thinks that some form of racial discrimination will prove “helpful” should thus tread cautiously, lest
racial discriminators succeed (as they once did) in using such language to disguise more invidious motives. Arguments
for the benefits of race-based solutions have proved pernicious in segregationist circles. Segregated universities
once argued that race-based discrimination was needed “to preserve harmony and peace and at the same time furnish
equal education to both groups.”

It is high time for the UK to reflect on this landmark decision across the pond and act to prevent the widespread
practice of discrimination by immutable characteristics. It is of the upmost importance that the UK remain committed
to meritocracy, equality and freedom to all, as the great classical liberal thinkers who occupied this island
championed for, let their legacy not go to waste.
