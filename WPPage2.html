The Christian case for a British style monarchy

Late Queen Elizabeth II walking with King Charles III

By: Henri Rossano | Durham University | 13/10/2023

A few weeks ago, we commemorated one year since the death of Queen Elizabeth II and King Charles III’s
accession to the throne. This commemoration was juxtaposed with the release of a YouGov opinion poll,
which showed that, among 18 to 24-year-olds, only 30% say that monarchy is “good for Britain”, compared
with 77% among over 65s. Although young people often change their opinions over time, this sharp
generational divide should ring, if not the death knell of British monarchy, then at least alarms bells
among its defenders.

Christians and religious people generally should see this as a potential threat, as republics do not have
a good track record when it comes to being friendly to religious practice. But, to give the devil his due,
perhaps a certain scepticism towards monarchy is warranted. After all, monarchs throughout history have not
always been of the same ilk as that of our late queen or even of our current king. As a Telegraph article put
it, the role has been filled by “two foreigners, a rake and a madman”. Some monarchs, who claimed to have
acceded to the throne by divine appointment, have turned out to be tyrannical towards their subjects, a
seemingly incompatible combination. What’s more, kings and queens in Christendom often accorded themselves
powerful positions within the Church and posed as strong defenders of faith, all the while engaging in bitter
and divisive conflicts with the Church. For instance, Philip IV, who as French king used the legitimist title
of Rex Christianissimus, fought with Pope Boniface VIII over the right to appoint bishops, among other things,
and ended up sending armed troops to depose him.

The conflict between monarchs’ behaviour and their stated Christianity puts into question the practice of
raising them to such heights. Is it God’s intention that human societies should organise themselves in such
a way? After all, did God not express to the Prophet Samuel His disappointment with the people of Israel’s
decision to replace Him with a king? And yet, it is my firm belief that informed reason and moral sense point
towards monarchy, more specifically British-style monarchy, as the most legitimate form of political system.
Although the Christian pre-suppositions involved in this approach may seem narrow-minded, they offer an insight
into the deep and indispensable meaning implied in monarchy, and are thus accessible for all.

The need for hierarchy

To a certain extent, monarchic structures come naturally to human societies. This is partly inscribed in human
biology, with extensive research demonstrating our biological ancestors’ proclivity for hierarchical organisation.
But this is also borne out historically, where most early societies - meaning generally societies from
pre-civilisational tribal units, through to the Sumerians and the establishment of the first civilisations,
up to approximately the fourth century B.C. - coalesced around single leaders. Even the Greek and Roman republics
that followed had the tendency to revert to a system where one man controlled the reins of power, often with
populist and dictatorial (in the modern sense of the word) characteristics, such as Pericles and Caesar. There
are of course exceptions and nuances to this broad-brush overview of human history, but by and large it remains
true that less complex and developed societies, that is to say societies that are closer to a ‘state of nature’,
structure themselves along single-headed hierarchical lines. This makes sense if one is to consider the fact
that societies in the past faced many more existential crises, much more often than modern societies do. Beyond
the need for someone to fill executive functions and to have a unified foreign policy, it was also a necessity
to have a quick and absolute response to war or plague.

British society today, and economically developed societies more generally, hardly face the same level of existential
threats. And yet, Britain has kept its old monarchical system. In fact, it is the only Western country not to have
changed its constitution in the last three hundred or so years (effectively since Cromwell’s Protectorate in the
1650s). And while some countries were founded on a rejection of such a system, their constitutions came to emulate
that same monarchical structure: for instance, the US was founded on the back of a revolution against the tyranny
of the British monarchy, yet the office of President was made to look like that of the king – in fact, Alexander
Hamilton advocated for the creation of an English-style monarchy, and George Washington could have delivered on
that intention as President if he hadn’t given up the office after two terms. Moreover, political rituals in
countries, which have shed their monarchies, often, still contain the same pomp and circumstance that would
characterise a traditional monarchy. All this is not said to disparage these countries that no longer have kings
or queens, or to expose them as hypocrites. It is instead an observation of the reality of the world as God created
it, whereby societies need a symbolic entity at the top, lest they crumble to the ground.

Hierarchy of values

The first reason for this is that a country needs a hierarchy of values. Like individuals, collectives must
judge and measure choices and decisions according to the principles which they have set out as worthy of
aspiration. These principles may evolve over time, and individuals may disagree with them, but they form the
fundamentals of what unifies the collective and what keeps it stable, thereby ensuring the ‘psychological health’,
of the group. But for a hierarchy of values to exist psychologically or symbolically, it must at least partly exist
in the society, such that improvement in the social hierarchy may be realised by adherence to these societal
values. Take hard work for example, a value that characterises many societies. For it to be recognised as valuable,
it must be emulated and embodied within the actual social hierarchy, such that hard-working behaviour is conducive
to an improvement in societal status. This will never be perfectly the case, there will always be unfairness and
cheating, but fundamentally, a society relies on the existence of a hierarchy indicating that there is something
of value worth striving for. Flatten out the hierarchy, as many egalitarians have tried to do, and you eliminate
that distinction between what is good and encouraged by the society and what is not, because no behaviour leads to
advancement in the real world. It is effectively declaring that there is nothing of value, except equality, which
necessitates the individual’s potential and initiative to be stymied.

Societies generally seem to have grasped the value of hierarchy, not least of them Britain. Its principles, values
and ideals, developed over many centuries, are enshrined at the top, represented by the monarch. Many of the ideas
which emerged out of the British tradition are now seen as universal: ideas of freedom, rights, and equality before
the law, which are now proclaimed as human rights by institutions like the UN are in truth derived in great part
from the Christian tradition, as it was formed in the conflicts, debates and practices that characterised British
history. In fact, as early as 1470, Sir John Fortescue was writing about the rights and privileges of Englishmen
in his Commendation of the Laws of England, commenting on how even the king could not trespass on someone’s property
without their permission. These values, along with others that are more unique to Britain, are all kept alive by
the fact that they are grounded in custom and tradition, both of which are represented by that constant presence
at the top of the hierarchy. It is key to Britain that there be two heads of state: one that changes according to
the public whims or the common zeitgeist, and another that remains above that, representing the timeless values
that characterise our country, and it is important that the two should not be confused.

This does not mean that these values are created at the top. As Christians, we believe that God is the source of all
that is good and true, and that He is the ultimate point towards which all good hierarchies point: He is the ‘King of Kings’.
This concept is well represented by the symbol of the great pyramid, as found on the back of the US dollar bill.
The idea is that the entity at the top is simultaneously an embodiment of all that the hierarchy aspires to and is
separate from the hierarchy. In this way, we do not believe that the monarch is God, but that he or she is at the
top of the social structure, while still subject to God and equal to all in the eyes of God, who is above all.
Symbolically, this is what the cross on the top of monarchs’ crown is meant to be a reminder of: that the monarch
has God’s blessing or that God’s hand lays on their head, like the Jewish yarmulke, but also that God is above them.
Moreover, part of the monarch’s role is to stand as a pointer towards God, both by virtue of his or her character
and by virtue of the nature of his or her office.

Monarchy and implied meaning

Small ‘r’ republicans would debate the need for a monarch to fill those functions, arguing instead for a more fluid
and fair hierarchy with a deserving entity at the top. After all, shouldn’t the people at the top earn their way
there? This is a fair and understandable point, which aims at letting the most competent people achieve higher status.
I would also be remiss if I didn’t say that republics can function very well and have been a good medium for spreading
freedom and equality throughout the world. In fact, since the foundation of the United States, republics have been
the standard that all new states aspire to, such that even regimes like the USSR and North Korea call themselves
republics to legitimise themselves. Nor am I advocating for republics to revert to monarchy; I think that if it
not undergirded by history and tradition, a monarchy would not last long. But I think that monarchy fits in better
with a Christian view of the world, for a couple of reasons.

To begin, human beings need to be reminded that they do not create everything. The central idea of a republic is
that the people have raised an individual to the heights of power by their own will and strength, and accordingly
may tear him down if they so desire. This is consubstantial with the idea that any legitimate authority must spring
from below, from the people, and may never be ordained by something higher. The problem with this is firstly that it
is a recipe for instability, since the people can easily claim that they are not satisfied with the system that they
have inherited and thus it loses all legitimacy in their eyes. One must only look at the writings of republicans like
Thomas Jefferson to see this manifest: for instance, he writes that the constitution should be completely revised,
tabula rasa, at every generational milestone, meaning every twenty years or so, so that the people aren’t ruled by
the ideas of their ancestors. To say that any legitimate authority must come from the people is tantamount to saying
that anything that gets in the way of the people is illegitimate and must be destroyed – it is no wonder that republics
often appear on the back of bloody revolutions. Another problem is that the idea of the general will is often difficult
to define, and can often turn into either the tyranny of an individual who claims to embody the general will, or the
tyranny of the majority.

But on a more profound level, this vision poses a problem to a Christian way of life because it is fundamentally a
prideful attitude. Indeed, the idea that we create our own systems ourselves, that there is no framework that is inherited
from God or from our ancestors who were God-believers, can quickly translate into the Nietzschean idea that we can create
our own values. This, after all, is the central idea of the story of the tower of Babel: humans want to reach the
heavens – a utopian aspiration – and to do so must build their own ethical framework. But as they try to reshape moral
reality, actual reality, portrayed as God because He is its creator, comes to foil their plans. A more humble attitude
is to see the world as a prolonged covenant between God and His people passed on from one generation to the next. This
idea underlies Burke’s conception of society: for him, society is a contract between past, present and future generations,
whereby we inherit certain systems, and we must try to preserve what is good within them so that we may pass them on to
the next generation. God is central to this view because He is meant to be the guiding hand as society progresses and
develops, such that it may be gradually shaped by His Divine Law. In this way, human beings do have natural rights
bestowed unto them by God and thus preceding society, but these natural rights cannot be understood by pure reason,
having instead to be discovered through human custom conforming to divine intent. Having a monarchy like Britain’s,
sustains that continuity which binds one generation to the next and represents the timeless values, which call society
towards perfection, towards God. In this way, that there are certain things which are beyond our control and that are
greater than us is a reminder of the humility with which we should approach the world as we understand it, and it
grounds us in reality because it establishes a basic value system inherited from people who were themselves grounded
in reality, which should consequently not be recklessly tampered with.

Finally, monarchy by nature tends towards the religious because it is a reminder of the immaterial realm of meaning
that undergirds and brings life to the physical world. Indeed, the idea that an individual can embody the nation is a
rebuke to the rationalistic view that matter is the only valid level of analysis through which to understand the world.
When atheist thinkers try to apply the same logic and method as the material world requires to religion, arguing that
the scientific method does not prove God, they often display a misunderstanding of the fundamental religious belief in
a spiritual realm, existing in consonance with the physical realm. As religious people, we believe that the self is a
physical being, but we believe that this physical embodiment of the self is a reflection, a symbol, of the spiritual idea
of the self. To put this in a clearer sense, an action from a scientific standpoint is just an interplay between parts
of matter, but we believe that this interplay of matter has some sort of meaning attached to it: the actions of giving
bread to a starving child on one side and taking it away from him on the other, may seem the same to a scientific
rationalist but we associate different meanings to them – the former represents something good while the latter does
not. Thus, we attribute specific meaning to certain physical realities, and it is thereby possible for a physical
reality to represent or embody a certain principle. All this is to say that a system of monarchy like Britain’s,
understands that political power is not threefold but fourfold: there are those powers that Montesquieu
identified - executive, legislative and judicial – but there is also the symbolic, which is often confused with
the executive, with often dangerous and divisive consequences. And it is this last form of power that the monarch
holds by representing and thus embodying the nation. Through this example we understand that we are not limited to
our fleshly existence but we are called to incarnate a metaphysical or immaterial principle like truth, beauty,
endurance or gentleness, the same way Christ incarnated the all-encompassing principle which we can only express
using words like God, the Logos, Being itself.
