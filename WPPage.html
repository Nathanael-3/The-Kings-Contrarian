The Star-Spangled Banner Should Remain Waving, Full Mast, O’er The Land Of The Free And The Home Of The Brave

US flags flying at half mast in front of the Statue of Liberty

By: Grant Wernick | Duke University | 23/10/2023

Americans are flag obsessed to the point of shocking the self-effacing British conscience. Just ask CNN
contributor Laura Beers, who claims her English husband detects “sinister overtones of interwar
dictatorship” in the way American students traditionally start the day—by pledging allegiance to the flag,
and to the republic for which it stands.

The US national anthem layers on the devotion even thicker than the pledge of allegiance. My friend Anna
from Oxfordshire can’t sing God Save the King but knows how the Star-Spangled Banner sounds belted out by
9,000 rabid Duke fans before tip-off at Cameron Indoor Stadium. The redcoats couldn’t touch our broad
stripes and brights stars at Fort McHenry two centuries ago, and she hears about it every time she attends
a college basketball game.

Americans feel in our bones that we have nothing to learn from the British, much less the British monarchy
(notwithstanding Prince Harry’s universally well-received musings, like when he declared the First Amendment
“bonkers”). However, to show the Stars and Stripes the respect they deserve, we Americans must swallow our
pride and look to the example of Buckingham Palace.

The royals follow the Union Jack protocols to a fault. One such protocol reserves the lowering of the flag to
half-mast for the death of a member of the royal family. Notoriously, they even initially refused to lower the
flag for Diana, who technically was not a princess at the time of her death. (Prince Phillip complained privately
of “great humiliation” when a compromise was reached.) In the Land of the Free, there is no need for a special
flag carveout for the monarchy. Nonetheless, we should be much more sparing in our use of this potent symbol
of national mourning.

I first noticed excessive half-masting in 2022 in North Carolina. Frequently the flag would be lowered without any
clear national tragedy. Combing through past North Carolina flag alerts, it appears many of the instances were
decisions by Governor Roy Cooper to commemorate the passing of a former member of the state legislature.
Admittedly, this decision is within the wide latitude granted to governors by the US flag code. But the passing
of a local politician, which has no national significance, is a poor reason to lower what is the ultimate
national symbol.

To his credit, Governor Cooper seems as conservative as Prince Phillip when compared to his counterpart in
New Jersey, Phil Murphy. At the beginning of August, I was attending a family reunion in a scenic seaside town
of the Garden State, when my bliss was interrupted by the sight of the American flag at half-mast over town hall.
I had not seen the latest news and could not discount the possibility of a mass shooting or some other heinous
event (though, in what should have been a hint, no private residences had elected to follow the government
building’s suit).

No large-scale tragedy of the sort had occurred. Rather, the lieutenant governor of New Jersey, Sheila Y. Oliver,
had suddenly passed away. In Executive Order Number 338, Governor Murphy mandated that US and New Jersey
state flags be flown at half-mast for one month to commemorate Oliver.

Governor Murphy’s desire to honor her life and service was completely understandable. It may even have been appropriate
to lower the US flag for a short period (though no more than 10 days, the amount of time allocated by the US flag
code for the death of the vice president). But to put 30 days in perspective, that was how long John F. Kennedy
was honored after being assassinated by the CIA (ahem, Lee Harvey Oswald). It was 20 days longer than the flags
were lowered after 9/11, the most lethal attack on civilians in our nation’s history. And it was nearly one month
longer than the four day order by Governor Murphy after the terrorist attack at Abbey Gate that claimed the lives
of 13 US servicemembers, during the disastrous evacuation of Afghanistan.

I understand that Governor Murphy deeply respected Ms. Oliver and considered her “a trailblazer and an icon,” but
honoring the memory of a deceased politician for three and half weeks longer than 13 young Americans, who gave
the last full measure of devotion, is obviously disgraceful.

There are better ways of honoring the passing of local figures than lowering the American flag. Leave it at its
rightful place, gallantly streaming o’er the ramparts, except after the most devastating tragedies. Doing so will
not make us more British, God forbid, but it will preserve our most potent symbol of national mourning for the
dates that deserve to live in infamy.
