What Are We To Make Of The News Of High Schools Withholding Notifications Of National Merit Awards?

Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology in Virginia, USA

By: Nathanael Kennedy-Leroi | King's College London | 03/09/2023

It is unjust that one should be penalised because of a desire for absolute equality, which, make no
mistake is the ultimate goal of these initiatives. It harms not only the person whose award was withheld
from them, but also those that the schools are supposedly trying to help. This teaches a generation that
hard work and excellence is pointless and instead egalitarian mediocrity is cherished.

Equality of opportunity necessarily leads to inequality of outcomes. Firstly, we are not born equal and
secondly, we use our free will and individual liberty in different ways. Some students may decide to work
incredibly hard to win National Merit awards, while others would rather engage with sports or a significant
social life. These decisions will all have consequences on the equality of outcomes. It is not compassionate
for a society to give students a false sense of competence, as Ben Shapiro’s catchphrase goes ‘facts don’t care
about your feelings,’ and neither should the allocation of National Merit awards.

At my college the opposite has been true to high schools, where instead of withholding merit from talented
students, grade inflation aims to reward mediocre students through boosting their grades to create better
grade equality. These anti-meritocracy initiatives will potentially greatly affect my future academic
decisions. As someone who is hoping to apply to law school in the US, the attempt to scrap the use of the
LSAT in favour of subjective diversity & inclusion criteria will likely play a major role in my decision.
