A Once Great Alliance Has A New Black Sheep

Russian President Vladimir Putin shaking hands with Kazkah President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev

By: Nathanael Kennedy-Leroi | King's College London

Russian foreign policy traditions can be contextualized as a linear path from the material to the social
and back to the material. It begins with the unique geographical implications that Russia faces, for example
the size of the nation, the fact that it has very few paths to the ocean, its harsh conditions and most
importantly for this article, its geographical weakness from the Ukrainian heartlands. This material geographic
uniqueness creates a culture of constructivism that materializes in Russian exceptionalism. Rhetoric associated
with Russia being destined to be a great power, harking back to its days of imperial expansion under the
Russian Empire and the USSR, led by ‘great’ political leaders like Peter the Great and Joseph Stalin come from
this geographic abnormality. This constructivist approach to foreign policy then influences the individual level
of FPA, where the Russian people associate its great power with strong autocratic leaders governing the country.
Despite years of persecution, famine, slavery and poverty, 51% of Russians still claim that they admire and like
Joseph Stalin. The association between great power and a great leader has allowed Putin to cement his role as a
dictator and to largely go unchallenged in his foreign policy prognosis. This focus on the individual level in
Russia then ends up culminating in a realist foreign policy tradition. Its geography, that constructs a great
power identity wielded by an autocrat means that Russia longs for a sphere of influence and remains constantly
paranoid of foreign incursions. This paranoia makes Russia deeply suspicious of its neighbors and the
international system at large, arguing that they are constantly duping the motherland. As Mearsheimer points
out, this paranoia grows exponentially as NATO expands up to the Russian border. Russia thus sees itself as a
lone wolf in the international system, therefore relying on power politics for its foreign policy
decision-making. These sentiments culminate in Russia regularly showing its military might to its neighbors,
Ukraine being the crescendo.

Comparing The Broader Post-Soviet Space

Ex-Soviet nations have taken wildly disparate paths in their attitudes to Russia and the international system
more broadly. The Baltic nations have opted for a primarily liberal approach to foreign policy, integrating
into both NATO and the EU and aligning themselves with the West. Now all three are members of the Eurozone and
have a strong democratic reputation. This group of nations represents one extreme, the other extreme are nations
that remain strongly aligned to Russia, rejecting any Western cooperation and having strong Kremlin-aligned
autocratic leaders. These nations include Belarus, Armenia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, and Tajikistan. These
nations for the most part have operated within the realist framework, realizing that their best option, both
for their leaders to stay in power and for the survival and health of their nations, is to align themselves
very closely with Russia. These five nations are all run by individual dictators, thus the foreign policy
decision-making is heavily linked to individual leaders, often acting out of the interest in the survival
of their regimes. More middle of the road nations that have adopted an approach of, at times Russian
alignment and at times Western alignment, include Ukraine, Moldova, Georgia and Azerbaijan. These nations
have all sought closer relations with the West but have often been either enticed away by Russia or in the
case of Ukraine and Georgia; military action has taken place. These nations have largely adopted a pragmatic
alternating theoretical approach to foreign policy that can only be highlighted through a comparison between
the various other post-Soviet space countries. These nations don’t adhere strictly to one theoretical
approach. Yet, for this article I will aim to address the diverging stances taken by two of Russia’s closest
allies, Kazakhstan and Belarus.

Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan is an oil rich Central Asian nation with a population of around 20 million people. It gained
independence from the USSR in 1991 and has remained heavily aligned to Russia for much of its independent
history. In fact, for much of this time it was widely considered as Russia’s second strongest ally after
Belarus. It is a member of the CSTO, which is the Russian response to NATO, and its president Kassym-Jomart
Tokayev was able to remain in power thanks to Russian backing after political turmoil. This stance however
was not one of affinity for Russia but of political pragmatism. Russia had a regional hegemony over Central
Asia, accounting for a huge amount of trade with Kazakhstan and for its political leaders as a way to
maintain power. This for the time being meant it was in the interest for Kazakhstani leaders to remain on
the side of Russia. Yet, after the invasion of Ukraine and since the rise of China, Kazakhstan has become
somewhat opposed to Russia for the first time. Kazakhstan sees Russia as a declining world power and the
former Russian president Dmitry Medvedev recently tweeted that Northern Kazakhstan was next in line for
a Russian invasion after Ukraine. For Kazakhstan these are troubling developments that have led to the
nation aligning with the West by sanctioning Russia, sending humanitarian aid to Ukraine and stating
that they will refuse to recognize the Luhansk and Donetsk ‘self-proclaimed’ regions. This for such a
strong Russian ally is a significant act of defiance. The reasoning behind such a move seems motivated
purely out of self-interest and a sense of fear. Classical realism assumes that individuals primarily
act out of fear or aggression and that this paradigm is then played out through domestic politics. It
would be safe to assume that Kazakhstani leaders are fearful that military action will be carried out
at their northern border, much like in Georgia and now Ukraine. The recent shift in attitude seems to
indicate a feeling that Putin is acting in an increasingly irrational way, likely due to fear of the
West, thus stoking up additional fear in the Kazakhstani leadership. The unprovoked invasion of Ukraine
now calls into question the extent to which aligning oneself to Russia is beneficial for leadership
survival on the individual level. Kazakhstan has furthermore utilized a situation where Russia is
increasingly weak, to misalign itself. The leadership knows that Russia is highly unlikely to invade
Kazakhstan at this moment in time, as it would mean that they would be fighting on two fronts thousands
of kilometers’ apart. Thus, due to the issues Russia is currently facing from the invasion of Ukraine,
Kazakhstan has been able to not only distance itself but also actively challenge its once greatest ally.

Belarus

Belarus on the other hand has had a drastically different approach, aligning itself even closer to Russia
and letting Russia station troops in the nation to then invade Ukraine. Belarus is a mid sized Eastern
European nation home to around 9 million people. It similarly gained independence from the USSR in 1991
and since 1994, has been governed by the Kremlin aligned Alexander Lukashenko, who recently began his
sixth term as president. Belarus is widely considered Russia’s greatest ally, siding with the nation
through thick and thin. Belarus has been sanctioned heavily alongside Russia after the invasion of Ukraine,
causing it to only align itself even further. For Belarus, their expression of classical realism is very
much the same as Kazakhstan but leads to different outcomes between the two nations. Classical realism’s
focus on domestic politics and human nature is highly important for a nation who has had a single
autocratic leader for all of its history. Lukashenko has built his political career around Soviet
nostalgia, gets significant support from Putin in his election campaigns, and Belarus as a nation is
highly dependent on trade and energy with Russia. For Lukashenko’s political survival, there is only one
state that would allow autocracy in Europe and that is Russia. Lukashenko knows that if he wants to
continue his political career and not be poisoned in the process, it is in his interest and because he
represents Belarus as a whole, in its interest, to remain as aligned with Russia as possible. Furthermore,
Belarus is on the border of Ukraine and already has Russian troops stationed within its territory,
thus any provocation on the part of Belarus makes a Russian invasion against the nation far easier than
a campaign against Kazakhstan, despite what Medvedev says. Since the invasion of Ukraine, Belarus has
deepened its ties even further, recently signing an agreement to allow Russian nuclear weapons to be
stationed in its territory. Similarly to Kazakhstan, these actions on the part of Belarus seem to be
driven by fear, but unlike Kazakhstan this fear has led to even closer cooperation between Russia and
Belarus. A key event in the international system i.e. the Russian invasion of Ukraine can lead to similar
reactions in terms of fear between two Russian allies in Belarus and Kazakhstan and yet two polar
opposite foreign policy responses. The Russian invasion of Ukraine has had significant implications for
Belarus as a neighbor of both nations, creating a situation where either further Russian support or
Russian defiance were potential options. Belarus opted for the former and has gone so far as to essentially
surrender its territorial sovereignty to Russia, allowing it to station troops and nuclear missiles
in the nation.

In Sum

Following the Russian invasion of Ukraine, the post-Soviet space has diverged markedly in highly interesting
ways. Once Russia’s second greatest ally, Kazakhstan has decided to distance itself with its northern
neighbour potentially realizing that the centre of power in Asia now lies in the hands of China. Belarus
on the other hand decided that its best approach was further Russian alignment, most probably for reasons
relating to Lukashenko’s political survival. Which strategy will play out the best? I will leave that to
the fate of history.
